What is film?

The illusion of motion

Film creates the illusion of motion through the rapid succession of individual frames recorded on film stock. This illusion is possible because of two quirks in our vision system:

Critical flicker fusion:
the human eye can only perceive up to around 50 separate images per second. When 50 or more images flash before our eyes in one second, the eye can no longer perceive the breaks between the images.
Apparent motion:
if a visual display changes quickly enough (i.e. once critical flicker fusion is achieved), the human eye will compensate for the change by perceiving continuous movement.

The mechanics of film

A camera is a light-tight chamber in which film is spooled from one reel onto another. In between the two reels, the film passes the aperture of the lens, where a shutter admits light intermittently, thus exposing each individual frame of film. The projector is basically an inverted camera.

Film stock consists of a transparent base coated with an emulsion containing light-sensitive chemicals. The first type of film base used to record films was cellulose nitrate, which was highly flammable. It has now been replaced with a safer polyester base. All plastic is subject to deterioration and so is motion picture film. Cellulose nitrate is very unstable and deteriorates quickly - the film turns brittle and shrinks with time. Modern polyester-based stocks are far more stable by comparison and are expected to last hundreds of years.

The film is exposed to light in the camera, causing a chemical reaction in the emulsion, during which small clusters of crystals are formed on the film. The exposed film is usually a negative image, meaning that the areas of light and dark are reversed. It then needs to be developed or printed in order to be projected on the screen.

Originally, the speed of shooting and projecting film varied because cameras and projectors were operated by a hand crank. Now the most commonly used speed is 24 fps because that was the slowest speed still allowing sufficient sound quality when synchronous sound was introduced to film in the 1920s. Film is shot and projected at 24 fps, but the projector shutter breaks the light beam once as the new image is slid into place and once while it is held in place, so each frame is actually shown twice, thus making it 48 images per second, which is enough to fool our eyes into perceiving a continuous moving image.

The stages of film production

Think of these stages as rough guidelines - they do not have to happen in order. A person can start storyboarding before they secure funding, and editing can start before filming is completely done. Likewise, a trailer is frequently created before filming is finished. In fact, with recent trends in cross-platform media development and renewed vertical and horizontal integration, the stages are less distinct and separate than ever.

  1. Development - at this stage, the creative team develops a treatment (or a summary of an idea) and begins seeking funding.
  2. Pre-production - at this stage, a storyboard and concept art are developed, and the production team begins budgeting and planning for production.
  3. Production - this stage includes everything that happens on set, including filming, sound-recording, set building, etc.
  4. Post-production - now the recorded material is assembled through the editing process, and special effects and refinements are added.
  5. Distribution - this stage involves creating a trailer and marketing materials, negotiating with theaters and other potential distributors, and supplying the finished film to exhibitors.
  6. Exhibition - finally, the film can be screened in theaters or streaming platforms, and DVDs and BlueRays are released.
